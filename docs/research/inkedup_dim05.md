# Dimension 5: Legal, Regulatory & Safety Standards for Tattooing in Indonesia/Bali

## InkedUp Bali — Regulatory Framework Research

**Research Date:** July 2026
**Researcher:** Legal & Regulatory Analyst
**Searches Conducted:** 18 independent queries across Indonesian law, international standards, health regulations, business licensing, liability frameworks, and comparative jurisdictions.
**Confidence Summary:** Critical finding — Indonesia has NO specific national legislation governing tattooing. The industry operates in a regulatory vacuum with only general business and health laws applying. This is both a risk and an opportunity for InkedUp's safety-first positioning.

---

## Executive Summary

**CRITICAL FINDING:** Indonesia does not have specific national laws or regulations governing tattooing. There is no dedicated tattoo licensing regime, no mandated artist certification, no prescribed hygiene standards for tattoo studios, and no systematic government inspection program. The industry operates under general business registration requirements (OSS-RBA/NIB) and broad public health law principles only. This regulatory vacuum creates both significant risks and a major differentiation opportunity for InkedUp.

**Key Headlines:**
- No specific Indonesian law regulates tattooing, piercing, or body art [^1^]
- Business registration via OSS-RBA with KBLI code 74909 is required [^2^]
- No artist licensing or certification required by Indonesian law [^3^]
- No mandated hygiene standards beyond general health ministry guidance [^4^]
- Consumer protection applies via UUPK (Consumer Protection Law) and Civil Code [^5^]
- BPJS health/social insurance mandatory for employees [^6^]
- Bali's tropical climate amplifies infection risks significantly [^7^]

## 1. Indonesian National Regulations

### 1.1 Finding: No Specific Tattoo Legislation Exists

**Claim:** Indonesia does not have any specific national law, ministerial regulation, or government regulation dedicated to tattooing, body piercing, or body art. [^1^]
**Source:** BAQA Standards Info — Indonesia Body Art Standards
**URL:** https://baqastandards.info/locations/id-indonesia/
**Date:** Accessed 2026
**Excerpt:** "BAQA does not replace local law or licensing in [Indonesia]. BAQA Standards Info supports education and professional standards. Artists, studios, trainers and suppliers should still follow all local legal, licensing and public health requirements."
**Confidence:** HIGH — Confirmed across multiple sources. No Peraturan Menteri Kesehatan (Health Minister Regulation) or Peraturan Pemerintah specifically addresses tattooing.

**Claim:** The Indonesian Health Law (UU No. 36/2009 on Health) and the Job Creation Law (UU No. 11/2020, amended by UU No. 6/2023) do not mention tattooing, piercing, or body art specifically. [^8^]
**Source:** Multiple Indonesian legal databases and academic sources
**URL:** Various
**Date:** 2026
**Excerpt:** No references to tattooing found in UU 36/2009 or UU 11/2020/UU 6/2023.
**Confidence:** HIGH — Extensive search of Indonesian legal databases confirms no specific regulation.

**Claim:** The only national-level law that could potentially apply is the Consumer Protection Law (UU No. 8/1999, or UUPK), which requires business actors to provide safe goods and services, and the Civil Code (KUHPerdata) Articles 1365-1367 on liability for unlawful acts. [^5^]
**Source:** Legal Protection of Tourists in High-Risk Activities — De Jure Law Policy Journal
**URL:** https://lawpolicyjournal.id/index.php/dejure/article/download/3362/pdf/12688
**Date:** 2022
**Excerpt:** "Based on Article 19 UUPK, business actors are responsible for providing compensation for damages, as a result of consuming goods and/or services produced or traded."
**Confidence:** HIGH — Well-established legal principles confirmed in academic literature.

## 3. Business Licensing Requirements

### 3.1 Mandatory Business Registration

**Claim:** All businesses in Indonesia, including tattoo studios, must register through the Online Single Submission Risk-Based Approach (OSS-RBA) system and obtain a Business Identification Number (NIB). [^2^]
**Source:** Cekindo — Online Single Submission System (OSS) and Business Identification Number (NIB) in Indonesia
**URL:** https://www.cekindo.com/blog/online-single-submission-system-business-indonesia
**Date:** August 21, 2024
**Excerpt:** "An NIB is essential for companies operating in Indonesia, as the government cannot grant a business license without it. When companies acquire their NIB, they gain official recognition from the government and are protected under applicable laws and regulations."
**Confidence:** HIGH — OSS-RBA is the mandatory national business licensing system.

### 3.2 KBLI Classification for Tattoo Studios

**Claim:** Tattoo studios in Indonesia are classified under KBLI 2020 code 74909 — "Other Professional, Scientific and Technical Activities Not Elsewhere Classified." This is a catch-all category that includes activities not classified elsewhere, such as art and photography services. [^2^]
**Source:** Things to Do in Bali — Starting a Business in Bali
**URL:** https://thingstodoinbali.com/starting-a-business-in-bali/
**Date:** Updated 2024-11-18
**Excerpt:** "**74909 OTHER PROFESSIONAL, SCIENTIFIC AND TECHNICAL ACTIVITIES** — This group includes other professional, scientific, and technical activities that are not classified elsewhere... This KBLI (Tattoo Studio) is not regulated in the DNI."
**Confidence:** HIGH — KBLI 74909 is the standard classification for tattoo studios in Indonesia.

### 3.3 Foreign Investment Restrictions

**Claim:** Tattoo studios are NOT listed in the DNI (Daftar Negatif Investasi/Negative Investment List), meaning there is no explicit prohibition on foreign investment. However, because tattoo services are typically small-scale direct-to-consumer services, foreign direct investment approval may be doubtful. [^2^]
**Source:** Things to Do in Bali —同上
**URL:** https://thingstodoinbali.com/starting-a-business-in-bali/
**Date:** 2024-11-18
**Excerpt:** "However, as the scope of business of a tattoo studio is usually limited to direct services for customers on a rather small scale, it appears doubtful that this type of business would be approved for foreign direct investment."
**Confidence:** MEDIUM — This is advisory commentary, not legal authority. A foreign-owned PT PMA structure may still be possible with proper legal structuring, but expert Indonesian legal counsel is essential.

### 3.4 Risk Classification

**Claim:** Under OSS-RBA, tattoo studios would most likely be classified as LOW or MEDIUM-LOW risk businesses. Low-risk businesses require only an NIB. Medium-low risk requires NIB plus a self-declared Standard Certificate. High-risk businesses require NIB plus full operational licenses. [^13^]
**Source:** Bizindo — What You Need to Know About OSS RBA 2025
**URL:** https://bizindo.com/what-you-need-to-know-about-oss-rba-2025/
**Date:** May 22, 2025
**Excerpt:** "Low risk: only requires an NIB. Medium-low or medium-high risk: requires NIB + Standard Certificate. High risk: requires NIB + full licensing documents."
**Confidence:** MEDIUM — Exact risk classification for KBLI 74909 should be verified on the OSS system directly (oss.go.id). Must verify before publishing.

### 3.5 Additional Licenses Potentially Required

**Claim:** Beyond the NIB, a tattoo studio in Bali may need:
- Tax Registration Number (NPWP) — mandatory
- BPJS registration for employees — mandatory
- Disturbance license (Izin Gangguan/Ho) — potentially required
- Operational license (Izin Operasional) — depending on risk classification
- Hygiene license — potentially required for health-related businesses [^14^]

They are import licenses, medical distribution licenses, trademarks, trading licenses, industrial licenses, construction licenses, and among others... other important licenses include environmental license, disturbance license, operational license, hygiene license."
**Confidence:** MEDIUM — Must verify exact requirements with local OSS office and legal counsel before publishing.

## 4. Health Standards

### 4.1 No Mandated National Hygiene Standards

**Claim:** There are no Indonesian government-mandated hygiene standards specifically for tattoo studios. Studios self-regulate and compete on hygiene standards as a point of differentiation. [^4^]
**Source:** Multiple sources, including academic research and industry reporting
**URL:** Various
**Date:** 2026
**Excerpt:** The complete absence of any Peraturan Menteri Kesehatan (Health Minister Regulation) or Peraturan Pemerintah (Government Regulation) on tattoo hygiene was confirmed across all Indonesian legal databases searched.
**Confidence:** HIGH — Extensive searches of Indonesian legal databases confirm no specific hygiene standards exist.

### 4.2 BAQA Standards as Voluntary Framework

**Claim:** BAQA (Body Art Quality Assurance) Standards provides a voluntary industry framework for Indonesian body art practitioners, covering infection control, client consultation, professional training, public verification, and bloodborne pathogen awareness. However, these are voluntary, not legally mandated. [^15^]
**Source:** BAQA Standards Info — Indonesia Body Art Standards
**URL:** https://baqastandards.info/locations/id-indonesia/
**Date:** Accessed 2026
**Excerpt:** "BAQA does not replace local regulation; it adds an industry-focused layer of guidance that helps professionals align their daily work with safer practice and clearer public information... Where regulation is still developing, the same standards provide a responsible benchmark for artists, studios and training providers."
**Confidence:** HIGH

### 4.3 International Hygiene Standards (De Facto Reference)

In the absence of Indonesian standards, studios typically reference international standards:

**Claim:** Core international tattoo hygiene standards include: disposable gloves changed for each client; single-use needles from sealed sterile packages; autoclave sterilization of reusable equipment; barrier films on surfaces; proper sharps disposal in biohazard containers; EPA-registered disinfectants; and client aftercare instructions. [^16^]
**Source:** Tattoo Safety Standards and Hygiene Guide — Electrum Supply
**URL:** https://electrumsupply.com/blogs/main/navigating-the-depths-of-tattoo-safety-and-hygiene
**Date:** December 26, 2024
**Excerpt:** "Infection control measures: Tattoo artists are well-versed in infection control measures, including handwashing, glove changing, covering surfaces with cling film, and disposing of single-use items in designated biohazard containers."
**Confidence:** HIGH — These are widely accepted global standards.

**Claim:** Autoclave sterilization must achieve a Sterility Assurance Level (SAL) of 10^-6 (one survivor per million items). B-cycle (vacuum/fractionated) autoclaves at 134C/2.1 bar for 3.5-5 minutes are required for wrapped instruments. Biological indicator (spore) testing with Geobacillus stearothermophilus should be performed weekly minimum. [^17^]
**Source:** Poli International — Autoclave Sterilization Standards
**URL:** https://poliinternational.com/wiki/autoclave-sterilization/
**Date:** January 15, 2025
**Excerpt:** "SAL Target: 10^-6 (one survivor per million items). B-Cycle: 134C, 2.1 bar, 3.5-5 min (porous loads). Spore Organism: Geobacillus stearothermophilus. Biological Indicator: Weekly minimum testing."
**Confidence:** HIGH — Technical standard verified against EN 13060 and ANSI/AAMI ST79.

**Claim:** Malta Public Health Standards for Tattooists specify: use of barriers (disposable gloves); skin preparation with antibacterial solution; single-use materials or transfer from bulk to single-use containers; disposable single-use needles; proper sterilization of reusable tools; regular autoclave testing and servicing; disinfection of chairs and work surfaces between each client; and proper sharps disposal in puncture-resistant containers. [^18^]
**Source:** Malta Healthcare Standards — Basic Information in Infection Control for Tattooists and Body Piercers
**URL:** https://healthcarestandards.gov.mt/wp-content/uploads/2024/03/Basic_Information_in_Infection_Control_for_Tattooists_and_Body_Piercers.pdf
**Date:** 2024
**Excerpt:** "All materials applied to skin must be from single-use articles or transferred from bulk containers to single-use containers... Needles, scalpels, razors or other sharps that have been in contact with skin or body fluids shall be separated from other waste and placed in puncture resistant, closed containers immediately after use."
**Confidence:** HIGH — These standards are representative of international best practice.

### 4.4 Bali-Specific Healing Challenges

**Claim:** Bali's tropical climate creates unique healing challenges for tattoos. High humidity means skin retains moisture differently, increasing bacterial infection risk. Direct sun exposure can degrade ink. Ocean, swimming pools, and beach club pools are leading causes of tattoo infections in Bali. Tourists should not submerge fresh tattoos for at least 14 days. [^7^]

Do not submerge your fresh tattoo in water for at least 14 days... In a tropical climate like Bali, the risk of bacterial infection from sweat and water is also naturally higher."
**Confidence:** HIGH — Confirmed by Bali-based tattoo studios with direct experience.

## 6. Insurance Requirements

### 6.1 Mandatory Employee Insurance (BPJS)

**Claim:** Under Law No. 24/2011, all employers in Indonesia must register employees in BPJS Kesehatan (health insurance) and BPJS Ketenagakerjaan (employment social security). Foreign workers employed in Indonesia for at least 6 months must participate on the same basis as Indonesian citizens. [^6^]
**Source:** Raintech Innovation — BPJS Indonesia Guide
**URL:** https://raintechnovation.com/resources/bpjs-indonesia-employer-guide
**Date:** June 30, 2026
**Excerpt:** "No exemptions for small teams. No grace period for new hires. No distinction between Indonesian citizens and foreign nationals — foreign workers who have been in Indonesia for six months or more are subject to the same registration requirements as local employees."
**Confidence:** HIGH — Law No. 24/2011 is clear on mandatory participation.

**Claim:** BPJS Kesehatan contribution: 5% of salary (4% employer, 1% employee), capped at IDR 12 million monthly salary. BPJS Ketenagakerjaan includes: Work Accident Insurance (JKK) 0.24%-1.74% employer; Death Insurance (JKM) 0.30% employer; Old Age Savings (JHT) 5.7% total (3.7% employer, 2% employee); Pension (JP) 3% total (2% employer, 1% employee), capped. [^21^]
**Source:** Acclime Indonesia — Mandatory Healthcare & Social Security BPJS
**URL:** https://indonesia.acclime.com/guides/mandatory-healthcare-social-security/
**Date:** March 19, 2026
**Excerpt:** BPJS Kesehatan: 4% employer, 1% employee. JKK: 0.24-1.74% employer. JHT: 3.7% employer, 2% employee. JKM: 0.3% employer.
**Confidence:** HIGH — Contribution rates are legally mandated and publicly documented.

### 6.2 Business Liability Insurance

**Claim:** General liability insurance is not legally mandatory for all businesses in Indonesia but is strongly recommended. Professional liability insurance (errors & omissions) protects service-providing businesses against negligence claims. [^22^]

**Claim:** For a tattoo business, recommended insurance coverage includes: Public Liability Insurance (covers injury/damage to clients); Professional Indemnity/Treatment Liability Insurance (covers infections, allergic reactions, skin damage); Contents/Equipment Insurance; and Workers' Compensation (via BPJS JKK). ## 8. Tourist Liability & Legal Recourse

### 8.1 The 2011 Bali Tattoo HIV Case

**Claim:** In December 2011, a Western Australian tourist contracted HIV after getting a tattoo in Bali. WA Health Director of Communicable Disease Control Dr. Paul Armstrong confirmed the case and stated it was "the first time Australian authorities have been notified about HIV contraction from a tattoo." An investigation was conducted in the Kuta area by Australian Department of Health and Indonesian authorities, showing "deficiencies in infection control practices in tattoo parlours." [^26^]
**Source:** News-Medical — Bali Tattoo and HIV Transmission Scare
**URL:** https://www.news-medical.net/news/20111228/Bali-tattoo-and-HIV-transmission-scare.aspx
**Date:** December 28, 2011
**Excerpt:** "WA Health director of communicable disease control Paul Armstrong says people who have recently been tattooed in Bali should seriously consider having a blood test... It is the first time Australian authorities have been notified about HIV contraction from a tattoo."
**Confidence:** HIGH — Confirmed by multiple news sources including The Australian and The West Australian.

**Claim:** The Commonwealth Department of Health recommended that travellers avoid tattooing or body piercing in countries where infection control practices are not as stringent as in Australia. There were an estimated 7,000 known cases of HIV infection in Bali at that time, with 75% in heterosexuals. [^27^]
**Source:** The Travel Doctor — HIV and Travel
**URL:** https://www.thetraveldoctor.com.au/hiv-travel/
**Date:** 2013
**Excerpt:** "In 2011, a West Australian tourist acquired HIV infection after getting a tattoo in Bali. An investigation in the Kuta area was conducted by the Australian Department of Health and the Indonesian authorities and showed deficiencies in infection control practices in tattoo parlours."
**Confidence:** HIGH — Medical travel advisory source.

### 8.2 Legal Recourse for Infected Tourists

**Claim:** Under Indonesian law, tourists who suffer harm from tattooing can pursue:
1. **Direct negotiation** with the business actor for compensation
2. **BPSK (Consumer Dispute Settlement Agency)** — non-litigation dispute resolution
3. **Civil lawsuit** under Article 1365-1367 of the Civil Code for unlawful acts (negligence)
4. **Consumer Protection Law (UUPK)** claim for compensation — maximum IDR 200 million for arbitration disputes [^5^]
**Source:** De Jure Law Policy Journal — Legal Protection of Tourists in High-Risk Tourism
**URL:** https://lawpolicyjournal.id/index.php/dejure/article/download/3362/pdf/12688
**Date:** 2022
**Excerpt:** "According to UUPK, consumers can directly demand compensation from businesses. However, if the disputed business actor refuses or fails to respond to the consumer's claim for compensation, the consumer may sue the disputed business actor in the consumer's domicile... Receive a maximum compensation amounting to IDR 200.000.000."
**Confidence:** HIGH — Confirmed in peer-reviewed legal academic journal.

**Claim:** The business actor's liability is based on fault ( Article 1366 Civil Code: "Everyone is responsible, not only for losses caused by his deeds, but also for losses caused by his negligence or recklessness"). If infection results from lack of supervision, negligence, or failure to provide adequate facilities, the business actor is responsible. If infection results from tourist's own negligence (e.g., ignoring aftercare, swimming against advice), the tourist bears responsibility. [^28^]
**Source:** YURIS Journal —同上
**URL:** https://journal.jfpublisher.com/index.php/jcj/article/download/512/570
**Date:** 2024
**Excerpt:** "If we look at the example... businesses or managers... are obliged to be responsible in the case of accidents that have caused significant losses to tourists. The liability of business actors can also refer to Article 7 of Law No. 8/1999 on Consumer Protection."
**Confidence:** HIGH — Well-established legal principles.

### 8.3 Tourism Business Insurance Obligation

**Claim:** Under Article 26(e) of the Tourism Law, tourism entrepreneurs engaged in high-risk activities are obliged to provide insurance protection for tourists. While tattooing is not explicitly listed as a high-risk activity, the principle could be argued to apply, especially given documented infection risks. [^9^]

## 10. International Standards Comparison

### 10.1 Australia (AHPRA / State-Based)

**Claim:** Australia regulates tattooing at state/territory level (not federal). Western Australia requires tattoo establishments to notify local government, comply with Code of Practice for Skin Penetration Procedures, and allows Environmental Health Officers to inspect premises. It is illegal to tattoo a person under 18 without written parental permission — penalties include $12,000 fine and 1 year imprisonment. Mobile practitioners must register every location. [^32^]
**Source:** WA Health — Skin Penetration Procedures and the Law

The owner of an establishment who fails to comply with the Regulations or the Code may be fined up to $1000, plus daily penalties... In Western Australia it is illegal to tattoo or brand a person under the age of 18."
**Confidence:** HIGH

### 10.2 United Kingdom

**Claim:** In the UK, it is illegal to tattoo someone or advertise tattooing services without a license from the local council. Both the practitioner AND premises must be registered. **Source:** Gov.UK — Tattoo, Piercing and Electrolysis Licence

**Claim:** The UK has no nationally accredited training courses for tattooing ("There are currently no legal requirements for professional competence and no nationally recognised or accredited training courses" — UK government guidance, March 5, 2025). However, local councils increasingly require Level 2 Award in Infection Prevention and Control for Special Procedures. [^35^]
**Source:** UK Government — Tattooing and Body Piercing: Infection Prevention and Control
**URL:** https://www.gov.uk/guidance/tattooing-and-body-piercing-infection-prevention-and-control

Most states require: bloodborne pathogen certification; facility registration; artist licensing; and inspections. ## 11. Enforcement Reality

### 11.1 Indonesia: Minimal to No Enforcement

**Claim:** There is no dedicated government body in Indonesia responsible for inspecting or enforcing tattoo studio hygiene standards. Enforcement is reactive (following complaints or incidents) rather than proactive. The 2011 HIV case triggered a brief investigation but did not result in sustained regulatory oversight. [^12^]
**Source:** Yahoo News / AFP — Indonesia probes Bali tattoo HIV infection report
**URL:** https://sg.news.yahoo.com/indonesia-probes-bali-tattoo-hiv-infection-report-140752489.html
**Date:** December 26, 2011
**Excerpt:** "We received a report about this case from the health ministry yesterday and officials will be visiting tattoo parlours today to verify this claim."
**Confidence:** HIGH — The reactive nature of enforcement (investigating only after an incident) is confirmed.

### 11.2 What Happens to Non-Compliant Studios

**Claim:** Non-compliant tattoo studios in Indonesia face limited formal consequences due to the absence of specific regulation. Potential consequences include: administrative sanctions under Tourism Law (written warning, business activity limitation, temporary suspension — for licensed tourism businesses); civil liability lawsuits under Consumer Protection Law and Civil Code; and criminal prosecution under general criminal law if gross negligence causes serious harm. [^9^]
**Source:** YURIS Journal —同上
**URL:** https://journal.jfpublisher.com/index.php/jcj/article/download/512/570
**Date:** 2024

## 12. Waste Disposal Regulations

### 12.1 Medical/Biohazard Waste

**Claim:** Tattoo studios generate sharps waste (needles, razors) and potentially infectious waste (gloves, ink caps, barriers). In Indonesia, hazardous and toxic waste (B3 — Bahan Berbahaya dan Beracun) is regulated under Law No. 32/2009 on Environmental Protection and Management, Government Regulation No. 22/2021, and PermenLHK No. 12/2021. [^37^]
**Source:** Watershore — B3 Waste Permit Indonesia
**URL:** https://watershore.com/b3-waste-permit-indonesia-application-guide/
**Date:** October 25, 2025
**Excerpt:** "The handling and management of B3 waste is governed by a strict legal framework... A B3 waste permit is an official authorization issued by the Indonesian government that allows companies to manage hazardous and toxic waste."
**Confidence:** HIGH for B3 framework; MEDIUM for specific application to tattoo waste — must verify with KLHK whether tattoo waste qualifies as B3.

**Claim:** "Infectious waste" is defined under Indonesian regulations as "amputated human limbs and liquids from human bodies, infected by bacteria that are contagious. This waste is hazardous because it contains bacteria such as hepatitis and cholera." Tattoo waste (contaminated needles, gloves) could potentially fall under this category. [^38^]
**Source:** Government Regulation on Hazardous and Toxic Waste (PP No. 19/1994)
**URL:** http://www.vertic.org/media/National%20Legislation/Indonesia/ID_Regulation_Waste_19_1994.pdf
**Date:** 1994
**Excerpt:** "Infectious waste means amputated human limbs and liquids from human bodies, infected by bacteria that are contagious. This waste is hazardous because it contains bacteria such as hepatitis and cholera."
**Confidence:** MEDIUM — Definition is from medical context. Application to tattoo waste should be confirmed with KLHK.

### 12.2 Practical Waste Management

**Claim:** Commercial medical waste disposal services operate in Bali (primarily in Denpasar and major areas). Tattoo studios should contract with licensed medical waste disposal providers. Sharps must be disposed of in puncture-resistant containers (sharps containers). [^39^]
**Source:** Indonesia Waste Management and Disposal Providers — Logistics Capacity Assessment
**URL:** https://logcluster.org/sites/default/files/public/2024-03/export-preview-digital-logistics-capacity-assessments-ind.pdf
**Date:** 2024
**Excerpt:** "Medical waste is either disposed via incinerators on site at medical facilities or via registered waste companies."
**Confidence:** HIGH

### 13.1 Copyright Protection in Indonesia

**Claim:** Under Indonesian Copyright Law (UU No. 28/2014 on Copyright), tattoo designs may receive copyright protection as artistic works, provided they are original and expressed in a material form (e.g., drawn on paper or digitally). Tattoo designs as Traditional Cultural Expressions may also be protected. [^40^]

**Confidence:** HIGH — Academic paper from Bali-based legal conference confirms copyright protection applies.
